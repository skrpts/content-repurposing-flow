## Brand Personality Traits

Our brand has four defining personality traits. Every piece of content should reflect at least two of them.

1. **Approachable** — We speak like a knowledgeable colleague, not a textbook. We use plain language, explain concepts without condescension, and make the reader feel welcome regardless of their expertise level.

2. **Direct** — We get to the point. We respect the reader's time. We say what we mean without hedging, padding, or burying the lead under three paragraphs of preamble.

3. **Knowledgeable** — We know our subject and it shows — through specificity, not jargon. We back up claims with evidence, use concrete examples, and never bluff.

4. **Occasionally witty** — We have a human voice. A well-placed bit of humour makes content memorable, but we never force it, and we never prioritise being clever over being clear.

## Vocabulary Preferences

| Use | Avoid |
|-----|-------|
| "Start" or "begin" | "Commence" or "initiate" |
| "Use" | "Utilise" or "leverage" |
| "Help" | "Empower" or "enable" |
| "Buy" or "choose" | "Invest in" (unless literally financial) |
| "Simple" or "straightforward" | "Seamless" or "frictionless" |
| "Improve" | "Optimise" (unless technical context) |
| "People" or "teams" | "Stakeholders" or "end users" |
| "Works with" | "Integrates with" (in non-technical content) |
| "Guide" or "walkthrough" | "Playbook" or "blueprint" |
| "Problem" | "Pain point" or "challenge" (when it's clearly a problem) |
| "Show" or "explain" | "Unpack" or "deep-dive into" |
| "We think" or "We believe" | "It goes without saying" |

**General rule:** If a simpler word exists and carries the same meaning, use the simpler word.

## Tone Spectrum Across Content Types

### Blog Posts
- **Tone:** Conversational, curious, engaging
- Write as if explaining something interesting to a sharp friend
- First-person plural ("we") is encouraged
- Short paragraphs, subheadings, and scannable structure
- Humour is welcome when it lands naturally

### Whitepapers and Reports
- **Tone:** Authoritative, measured, evidence-based
- Third person is acceptable; first-person plural is also fine
- Longer paragraphs are permitted, but vary the rhythm
- Cite sources. Use data. Avoid unsupported superlatives ("the best", "the fastest")
- Still plain English — authoritative does not mean stuffy

### Social Media
- **Tone:** Casual, punchy, personality-forward
- Sentence fragments are fine
- Emojis: sparingly, and only when they add meaning
- Questions and direct address ("you") work well
- Never be sarcastic about competitors, customers, or industry trends

### Product and Help Content
- **Tone:** Clear, patient, task-focused
- Second person ("you") throughout
- Short sentences, numbered steps for procedures
- Anticipate confusion points and address them proactively
- Celebrate the user's progress where appropriate ("That's it — you're all set")

## On-Brand vs Off-Brand Examples

| On-brand | Off-brand |
|----------|-----------|
| "Here's how to set up your first project in under five minutes." | "Initiate your onboarding journey with our streamlined setup wizard." |
| "We got this wrong. Here's what we're doing about it." | "We apologise for any inconvenience caused during this transitional period." |
| "This feature saves you roughly two hours a week." | "This game-changing feature will revolutionise your workflow." |
| "Not sure where to start? We've got a guide for that." | "Leverage our comprehensive knowledge base to optimise your experience." |
| "We've been building this for six months. It's finally ready." | "We're thrilled to announce the launch of our next-generation platform." |

## Audience-Specific Adjustments

### Technical Audience
- Precision matters — use correct terminology, do not oversimplify
- Show, don't tell: code snippets, architecture diagrams, and worked examples carry more weight than adjectives
- Skip the marketing framing — they will see through it immediately
- Respect their time: lead with the technical detail, add context after

### Non-Technical Decision Makers
- Focus on outcomes and business impact, not implementation details
- Use analogies where they genuinely clarify (avoid forced metaphors)
- Keep paragraphs short, use bullet points for key takeaways
- Quantify where possible ("reduces review time by 40%")

### New Users and Beginners
- Assume nothing — define terms on first use
- Use screenshots and step-by-step instructions
- Offer reassurance ("Don't worry if this looks complicated — we'll walk through it together")
- Avoid overwhelming with options; guide them through the recommended path first

### Existing Customers
- Acknowledge their experience ("You're already familiar with X, so this will be quick")
- Focus on what is new or changed, not what they already know
- Be transparent about breaking changes or required actions
- Reward loyalty with early access language ("As an existing customer, you get first look at...")
